LLC vs Sole Proprietorship: Which Is Better for Taxes in 2025?

One of the most common questions from freelancers and small business owners is whether to operate as a sole proprietor or form an LLC. The short answer: for most people just starting out, the tax difference is smaller than you think — but it matters more as your income grows.

Here's a clear breakdown of how each structure is taxed in 2025, and when switching to an LLC actually saves you money.

1. The key tax difference

Here's the most important thing to understand upfront:

By default, a single-member LLC is taxed exactly the same as a sole proprietorship. Both report income on Schedule C and pay the same 15.3% self-employment tax. The difference only appears when you make a special tax election.

This surprises many people. Forming an LLC does not automatically reduce your taxes. The LLC's main benefit at the start is liability protection — your personal assets are shielded from business debts and lawsuits. The tax advantages come later, through an S corp election.

2. How sole proprietorship taxes work

As a sole proprietor, your business and personal taxes are combined. There is no separate business tax return.

  • Report all income and expenses on Schedule C (attached to Form 1040)
  • Pay self-employment tax of 15.3% on net profit
  • Pay federal income tax at your personal bracket rate
  • Deduct half of self-employment tax from gross income
Example: Net profit of $80,000. Self-employment tax = $80,000 × 0.9235 × 15.3% = $11,302. You deduct $5,651 (half) before calculating income tax. Simple, but expensive at higher income levels.

3. How LLC taxes work

A single-member LLC is what the IRS calls a "disregarded entity" — meaning for tax purposes, it does not exist separately from you. The default treatment is identical to a sole proprietorship.

4. The S corp election: where it gets interesting

An LLC can elect to be taxed as an S corporation by filing Form 2553 with the IRS. This is where real tax savings can happen — but only once your profit is high enough to justify the added complexity.

How the S corp election reduces taxes

With an S corp election, you split your income into two parts:

  • Reasonable salary — subject to payroll taxes (15.3% split between you and the "company")
  • Distributions — the remaining profit, which is not subject to self-employment tax
Example of S corp savings: Net profit $150,000. As a sole proprietor, you pay SE tax on the full amount = ~$21,195. With S corp election, you pay yourself a $80,000 salary (SE tax = ~$11,304) and take $70,000 as a distribution (no SE tax). You save approximately $9,891 per year — minus the added cost of payroll administration (~$1,500-3,000/year).
Important: The IRS requires you to pay yourself a "reasonable salary" — you cannot pay yourself $1 to avoid all payroll taxes. The IRS actively scrutinises this. Most tax professionals recommend the S corp election only when net profit exceeds $60,000-$80,000 per year.

6. Which should you choose?

Choose sole proprietorship if:
  • You're just starting out and testing an idea
  • Net profit is under $40,000/year
  • You want zero setup cost and paperwork
  • Your business has minimal liability risk
  • You prefer maximum simplicity
Choose LLC if:
  • You have real liability exposure (clients, contracts, physical products)
  • Net profit is above $60,000 and growing
  • You want to elect S corp status to save on SE tax
  • You want more professional credibility
  • You plan to take on partners or investors later

The honest bottom line

If you're earning under $60,000 in net profit, forming an LLC saves you nothing on taxes — the default treatment is identical to a sole proprietorship. The LLC's value at that stage is purely liability protection.

Once you're consistently earning $80,000+ in net profit, the S corp election through an LLC can save $5,000–$15,000 per year in self-employment taxes. At that point, the setup and payroll administration costs are worth it.
